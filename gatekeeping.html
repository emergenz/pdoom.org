Common misconceptions about AI research

AI research is only for PhD students.
Selection bias. Society agreed on arbritrary metrics for choosing PhD students. If you get paid to do research for three years, chances are high that you know a lot about your field by the end.
All AI researchers have maths degrees. I have no chance of becoming an AI researcher.
Selection bias. You will be surprised when you realise how little math is required to understand 99% of AI research papers, and perplexed by the irrelevance of the remaining 1%. If you don't believe me, I asked Jürgen Schmidhuber. His answer: If you understand the Leibnitz rule, you have all the maths prerequisites for AI research. I posit that you can get a long way (professorship) even without that.
All AI researchers I know are academic beasts. My grades have proven that I don't have any chance.
Selection bias. Many AI researchers on twitter are academic idiots. Grades have no meaning besides gatekeeping. I barely passed analysis. In fact, I miserably failed my machine learning exam. Nobody should give a fuck.
I have x research papers, but someone I know has x+y|y>0. I am not competitive enough.
The academic system is truly rigged. You become a successful researcher in academia by extending research directions by picking low-hanging fruits. You will not get a medal for pointing out the stupidity of the research direction. (The most you'll get is a shitstorm by angry 'established intellectuals' who cannot face the truth)
But I don't even know how to code, and tbh I am not motivated enough to learn it.
Get a $20 ChatGPT Plus subscription and use GPT-4 to code your research ideas. I posit that this is the fastest, most democratic and intuitive way of learning how to code. At first, most of the code will appear to be magic to you, but the more you prompt, the more proficient you will get at coding (without having had to 'learn' it first). The truth is, that most 10x programmers mostly prompt instead of coding (with geohot being a notable exception, but he will come around eventually).
