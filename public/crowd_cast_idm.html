Research About Supply Join

We train an inverse dynamics model on crowd-cast's paired screencast-action data that recovers key presses, mouse clicks, cursor movements and scroll events from raw video. Trained exclusively on macOS, it generalizes to Windows and Linux.

1

Environment maxxing until AGI??

The frontier is spending billions on handcrafted RL environments hoping to lengthen the task horizon to general intelligence. Meanwhile, a billion people are producing months-long economically valuable trajectories of digital work, a data trove that remains entirely uncaptured. We capture exactly this data in form of action-annotated screencasts, and subsequently behavior-clone models, lengthening their task horizon from hours to weeks, and months.

Previously, we openly released over 600 hours of raw screencasts of AGI research. However, raw screencasts alone are not enough to behavior-clone models. With crowd-cast, our open-source recording tool, we started capturing paired data: screencasts with synchronized keylogs and mouse movement. To date, we have collected over 2500 hours of paired recordings across dozens of contributors.

The crowd-cast recordings are high in quality and span months of continuous work on single projects. However, they are limited in breadth and diversity. The internet, on the other hand, hosts thousands of hours of screencasts covering a far broader distribution of professions, applications and operating systems. To tap into this larger but unlabeled trove, we need to recover the actions behind every frame. In this post we present the first version of our inverse dynamics model, which does exactly that.

2

Inverse Dynamics Model for general screencasts

When you watch a screencast, you can see text appearing in an editor, a cursor clicking a button, a page scrolling. The actions that produced these changes are recoverable: a human watching the recording can infer what was typed, what was clicked, and where the cursor moved. An inverse dynamics model does exactly that. Trained on crowd-cast's paired data (where we have both the video and the ground-truth actions), our model learns to recover the actions from the pixels alone.

Given a short window of consecutive screen frames, the model outputs a sparse list of user actions: key presses, mouse clicks, mouse movements and scroll events. While most frames have no action, some have several, and the model only emits the events it detects. We deliberately avoid per-frame action/no-op classification. At 5 fps, ~70% of frames have no associated input event; a naive per-frame classifier collapses to "always predict no-op" and gets 70% accuracy with zero useful signal. The sparse formulation sidesteps this entirely: the empty list [] is a valid response for clips where nothing happened.

The action space stays close to raw OS input events:

  • KeyPress: key name + modifiers (e.g. Cmd+S), attributed to the frame where the visual effect appears
  • MouseClick: button only (Left / Right / Middle), one per press event. Position is implied by the preceding MouseMove sequence
  • MouseMove: per-frame relative displacement (dx, dy) as signed integers on a 0-1000 per-axis normalized scale
  • MouseScroll: direction and magnitude

2.1

Data processing

Each crowd-cast recording consists of a screen-captured mp4 and a paired msgpack keylog containing raw OS-level input events with microsecond timestamps. We process these into training clips as follows:

  1. Frame extraction. We downsample each recording to 5 fps and resize to 1280x720. crowd-cast records a black screen when the focused application is not in the user's predefined recording list. These black frames carry no visual information and have no associated actions, so we detect and drop them. To reliably identify black frames, we crop the top 15% of the screen first, removing the macOS menubar, which would otherwise prevent fully-black detection even on unrecorded applications. We then slide a 10-frame window with stride 5 across each recording (50% overlap). At 5 fps, roughly 70% of the 2-second windows contain no user action at all. We keep only 5% of these zero-action clips as negative examples, enough for the model to learn that [] is a valid response without dominating the training loss.
  2. Action extraction. Raw OS-level keylog events are converted into the four emitted action types. Modifier keys never emit standalone; they are tracked as held state and prefixed to the details of the next non-modifier KeyPress. For example, holding Cmd+Shift then pressing P yields one event with details "Cmd+Shift+P". MouseMove deltas are signed integers on a per-axis [-1000, +1000] scale, where ±1000 corresponds to a full screen-width (dx) or screen-height (dy) traversal. MouseScroll magnitude is normalized the same way. Multiple raw events often land on the same frame after downsampling; we coalesce scrolls and mouse movements by summing their per-frame vectors. We index action $$a_{i}$$ as the action that led to the change in frame $$f_{i+1}$$.

We use a snapshot of the crowd-cast dataset from May 19, 2026, comprising ~18,000 recording segments across ~3,800 sessions. After processing, this yields approximately 1.5M training clips and 210K validation clips, split at the session level with zero overlap.

2.2

Model and training

We fine-tune Qwen3-VL-8B with LoRA applied to both the language model (attention + MLP projections) and the vision encoder (QKV, output projection, MLP). Frames are interleaved with text labels ("Frame F00:", "Frame F01:", ...) in the input sequence, giving the model text-space anchors across the 10 images. The model receives the frames and a task prompt as input and produces a JSON array of actions as output. Only the assistant response contributes to the loss.

At the pixel budget used for training, each frame produces 480 visual tokens after Qwen3-VL's spatial merge. A 10-frame training sample totals ~5,300 tokens: 4,800 visual tokens, ~500 for the prompt and interleave labels, and ~110 for the output (varying with the number of actions).

3

Experiments

3.1

Evaluation setup

We manually curated 44 macOS productivity clips (5 seconds each at 5 fps) covering keystroke-heavy, click-heavy, scroll/drag, hotkey and mixed workflows, with hand-verified ground-truth actions. Each ground-truth action is annotated with a visibility class: visible (~91%, clear visual change in frames), inferable (~4%, deducible from context), or not predictable (~5%, no visual evidence at this frame rate). Only visible and inferable actions count toward the score.

Each clip is 25 frames, but the model only sees 10-frame sequences during training. Thus, at inference we run four overlapping windows (stride 5, margin 2) and merge predictions across non-overlapping scoring zones so each frame's prediction comes from exactly one window. We match predictions to ground truth with a per-type frame tolerance. For KeyPress and MouseClick, we allow a 5-frame tolerance since the visual effect of a press or click can be delayed by several frames. For MouseScroll and MouseMove, we require an exact frame match: their GT is aggregated per-frame, so any tolerance would introduce significant complexity due to the risk of double-counting.

We report per-type precision, recall and F1. For KeyPress, a prediction is correct if the right key (including modifiers) was predicted on the right frame. For MouseClick, the button must match. For MouseMove and MouseScroll, F1 measures detection only: did the model predict movement or scroll on the correct frame? To measure MouseMove quality beyond detection, we additionally report R² between predicted and ground-truth displacement vectors, and cosine similarity for directional alignment.

3.2

Results

Our fine-tuned 8B model achieves F1=0.855 at 5,000 training steps, surpassing the strongest zero-shot model, Gemini 3.5 Flash, by +12 points on this task. On MouseMove, the trained model reaches R²=0.66 with a median cosine similarity of 0.987, meaning predicted cursor displacements closely track both the direction and magnitude of ground truth.

Without fine-tuning, even the best zero-shot model leaves significant room for improvement, and most open models cluster at F1 0.34-0.42. We observed two failure modes across nearly all models: they are competent at single-frame grounding and OCR but struggle with multi-frame tracking, and they severely underpredict, missing the majority of actions. Gemini 3.5 Flash, GPT 5.5 and Kimi K2.6 had thinking enabled, while Gemma 4, Qwen3-VL and our fine-tuned version were evaluated without.

3.5

Ablations

We ablate training decisions by changing one variable at a time against our final recipe.

Config F1 R² cos
Final recipe (8B, r=256) 0.86 0.66 0.99
2B model 0.86 0.61 0.97
4B model 0.84 0.55 0.91
No vision-LoRA 0.84 0.64 0.76
No interleave 0.77 -0.03 0.57

Removing interleaved frame labels materially affects all metrics, dropping F1 by 9pp and destroying mouse prediction. Without text anchors between frames, the model loses track of frame identity and enters repetitive prediction loops.

Model size, LoRA rank and vision-LoRA have no measurable effect on action detection. However, smaller model sizes and removing vision-LoRA degrades mouse prediction. Similarly, mouse direction accuracy scales monotonically with LoRA rank.

4

Out-of-distribution generalization

The model was trained exclusively on macOS paired data from crowd-cast. All training recordings come from macOS users running applications like Cursor, Firefox, Preview and Terminal. We evaluated the same model on Windows and Linux screencasts without any adaptation.

What generalizes. Mouse movement and click detection transfer well across operating systems. Cursor behavior looks similar regardless of OS: the model tracks the cursor position from pixel evidence, and the visual effect of a click (UI element activating, page navigation, modal appearing) is largely OS-agnostic.

What breaks down. Keyboard shortcuts are the most predictable failure: the model predicts Cmd+key instead of Ctrl+key on Linux and Windows, having never seen the Ctrl modifier during training. Write density also degrades on synthetic or tutorial-style content where the editing pattern (e.g. code being typed character-by-character in a tutorial) differs from the real productivity work in our training set, where editing is bursty and interspersed with long pauses.

These failures are predictable and correctable. The Cmd/Ctrl confusion is a pure vocabulary issue that disappears with cross-platform training data. The write-density gap narrows as the training distribution broadens beyond macOS productivity workflows. crowd-cast now runs on all three operating systems, and cross-platform paired data collection is underway.

5

crowd-cast on every platform, and what comes next

We have shown that an inverse dynamics model trained on macOS paired data generalizes surprisingly well to Windows and Linux screencasts. The obvious next step is to close the remaining OOD gap by collecting paired data on these operating systems too. As of today, crowd-cast runs natively on macOS, Windows and Linux.

With cross-platform support, anyone can now contribute to the dataset. We are paying participants to record their normal work sessions using crowd-cast. You are not asked to do tasks for us. You record yourself doing your own work that you would be doing anyway. If you are interested, apply here or check the live dashboard to see collection progress.

More paired data across all three operating systems will make the IDM better. A better IDM lets us action-annotate the millions of hours of unlabeled screencasts that already exist on the internet. And action-annotated screencasts at that scale are what we need to behaviour-clone models that can do real, long-horizon digital work.
